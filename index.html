Opening-day story

1. The Opening Day Story

A modern terminal with a huge system behind it

Heathrow Terminal 5 was presented as a major modern airport terminal designed to offer luxury, style, and world-class service. It was a very large and complex project involving many IT applications, vendors, interfaces, computers, connected devices, and a huge baggage-handling system.

The opening day failed badly

When Terminal 5 opened in 2008, problems began early. Staff and passengers had difficulty finding car parks and navigating the new layout. Staff delays and confusion meant check-in counters opened late, long queues formed, and passengers waited for luggage processing.

The baggage system became the crisis point

The baggage handling system began to fail, conveyor belt problems appeared, and the system could not handle the baggage flow. Later in the day, baggage check-in was suspended and many passengers flew without their bags.

The consequences spread quickly

British Airways cancelled flights on opening day and more flights in the following days. A baggage backlog developed, passengers were frustrated, and the airline faced refunds, compensation costs, and reputation damage.

Core idea

This was not only an operational inconvenience. Terminal 5 was a public-facing engineering and project system. When a complex system launches without enough readiness, training, risk planning, communication, and stakeholder response, the public is harmed.

Opening Day Failure Timeline

  1. Navigation confusion: Staff and passengers struggled to locate car parks and navigate the terminal.
  2. Late check-in: Check-in counters opened late because staff were delayed and confused.
  3. Queues formed: Passenger waiting times increased and pressure built across the terminal.
  4. Baggage issues appeared: Conveyor and baggage-system problems disrupted luggage flow.
  5. Baggage check-in suspended: The system could not process luggage properly.
  6. Passengers flew without luggage: Some flights departed without passengers' bags.
  7. Flights were cancelled: British Airways cancelled flights on opening day and after.
  8. Aftermath followed: Compensation, reputation damage, and inquiry followed the failure.

Why it is ethics

2. Why This Became an Ethics Case

This case is ethical because engineering and project decisions affected the public directly. The failure caused passenger inconvenience, long queues, missing baggage, cancelled flights, poor communication, loss of trust, financial costs, and reputation damage.

The ethical issue was not intentional fraud like Volkswagen. Instead, the ethical concern was negligence or poor responsibility in project execution. A project can be technically impressive but still ethically weak if it launches before it is operationally ready.

Big Bang Launch Approach

British Airways moved many operations into the new terminal at once. When problems started, there was little room to recover. A phased launch with fewer flights first would have reduced risk.

A large public system should not expose passengers to avoidable disruption when a safer phased approach is possible.

Weak Risk and Crisis Management

⭐ Most Important

The baggage system failure, queues, car park confusion, security screening delays, and layout confusion were not handled well. Contingency planning and crisis response were weak.

Ethics meaning:

Organizations have a duty to plan for predictable failures, especially in public infrastructure projects.

03

Poor Communication

Passengers reported little help, poor announcements, and weak information on monitors or websites.

Ethics meaning:

When users are affected, communication is not optional. It is part of accountability and respect for stakeholders.

04

Insufficient Training and Logistics Preparation

Staff were not prepared enough for the new terminal's operations. Some staff reportedly did not know how to unload processed baggage properly, contributing to overload and confusion.

Ethics meaning:

A system is not ready if the people operating it are not ready.

05

Implementation and Performance Planning Problems

Issues included software filter problems, message/server capacity limitations, and baggage testing that did not fully represent opening-day capacity.

Ethics meaning:

Testing must represent real operating conditions, not only controlled scenarios.

06

Ignored Stakeholder Concerns

Staff and unions raised concerns that the terminal was not ready, but these concerns were ignored.

Ethics meaning:

Ethical project management requires listening to people who understand and will operate the system.

External Pressure and Public Perception

Management felt pressure to open on the planned deadline and improve public perception of Heathrow and British Airways. This pressure contributed to opening despite risks.

Ethics meaning:

Deadlines and reputation should not override readiness and public responsibility.

Safety-critical software

1. Story

Ariane 5 Flight 501 was launched by the European Space Agency in 1996. Shortly after liftoff, the rocket malfunctioned, deviated from its intended flight path, experienced extreme stress, and was destroyed. The payload was not the main cause of the disaster. The failure came from software inside the rocket's inertial reference system.

The key background is that Ariane 4 had been successful for many years, so parts of its software were reused in Ariane 5. However, Ariane 5 was not simply another Ariane 4. It had different hardware, different flight behavior, and could produce values outside the safe range expected by the old software. The team became overconfident because the old software had worked before.

The technical failure came from a data conversion problem. A large 64-bit floating-point value was converted into a 16-bit signed integer. The value was too large for the smaller data type, causing overflow and corrupted data. The flight computer then used this invalid data and tried to correct the rocket's trajectory incorrectly, which caused the rocket to tilt and fail.

Testing did not catch the problem because the tests did not use realistic Ariane 5 flight values large enough to trigger the overflow. The code had been safe in Ariane 4 because Ariane 4 did not produce values that exceeded the old range, but Ariane 5 did.

The backup system also failed because it used the same identical software as the primary system. This means the backup repeated the same software fault instead of protecting against it. The redundancy was useful against some hardware failures, but weak against common-mode software failure.

This is an engineering ethics case because safety-critical software must be revalidated whenever the system changes. The disaster was not only a small programming bug. It reflected unsafe code reuse, unrealistic testing, weak redundancy, poor risk management, and an organizational culture that trusted past success too much. The main lesson is that reused software is not automatically safe in a new system. Engineers must test real conditions, check boundary cases, handle failures safely, and question old assumptions.

Medical-device safety

1. Story

Therac-25 was a radiation therapy machine made by AECL Medical. It was designed to treat cancer patients using high-energy electron beams or x-rays. Unlike older machines, Therac-25 relied heavily on software instead of physical hardware interlocks. This made the software safety-critical because wrong software behavior could directly expose patients to dangerous radiation.

The first major victim discussed in the video was Katie Yarborough. She went for a normal cancer treatment expecting a small prescribed dose, but during treatment she felt a burning sensation and told the technician, "You burned me." The technician believed this was impossible. Later, her injuries showed that she had received a massive overdose, not the intended safe dose.

The problem did not stop after one patient. Similar accidents happened in different hospitals. Patients reported burning, electric shocks, pain, or strange sensations during treatment. Some suffered severe injuries and some died. Doctors, physicists, and operators became suspicious because the injuries looked like radiation overdoses, but AECL repeatedly claimed that such overdoses were impossible.

A major issue was the machine's error handling. Operators often saw vague error messages such as "malfunction" followed by a number. The meanings of these codes were not clearly explained in manuals. Operators became used to these errors and could press a key to proceed without understanding whether the error was harmless or deadly.

One famous error was Malfunction 54. It was eventually reproduced by Dr. Fritz Hager and staff after a serious accident. They discovered that if an operator quickly edited the treatment settings within the few seconds while magnets were physically moving into position, the software did not properly detect the change. The screen could show one treatment setup while the machine was physically configured differently, allowing an unfiltered high-energy beam to hit the patient.

Another later problem involved arithmetic overflow in a software task that checked the turntable position. The counter could overflow back to zero, making the system think there was no error and allowing treatment to proceed incorrectly.

The deeper ethical issue is that Therac-25 trusted software too much and removed or reduced hardware safety interlocks used in earlier machines. AECL's safety analysis did not properly examine the software, even though the software controlled a life-critical medical device. The software was based on older code, written by one unidentified programmer, and lacked strong independent documentation and review.

The case became visible through repeated patient injuries, doctors and physicists reporting suspicious radiation burns, operators comparing incidents, and finally the reproduction of Malfunction 54. The FDA declared the device defective and demanded corrective action. Later fixes included software changes and hardware safety features, such as a dose-per-pulse monitor that could stop dangerous doses even if software failed.

The main lesson is that in medical and safety-critical systems, software must never be trusted blindly. Engineers must design for real user behavior, clear error messages, independent software review, hardware fail-safes, proper testing, and fast accountability when patients report harm.

Tacoma Narrows Bridge Collapse

Bridge Failure Logic

1. Case in One Clear Story

Tacoma Narrows Bridge opened in 1940 as a long suspension bridge. To reduce cost and material, engineers used a very slender plate-girder design instead of a deeper, stiffer truss design. This made the bridge elegant and economical, but also very flexible.

Even before collapse, the bridge moved noticeably in moderate winds and was nicknamed "Galloping Gertie." This movement was an early warning sign. Four months after opening, wind caused the bridge to move violently. The important point is that the bridge did not fail simply because it was too weak for gravity loads. It failed because its shape and flexibility made it unstable under wind.

The final collapse was mainly due to torsional flutter, meaning the bridge twisted as wind forces amplified the motion. This is different from simple vertical resonance. The bridge showed that a structure can look efficient and strong in one sense, but still fail if engineers underestimate dynamic environmental forces.

This case teaches that engineers must design for real-world conditions, not only ideal calculations. Cost-saving and innovation are acceptable only when public safety is still protected.

Hyatt Regency Walkway Collapse, Kansas City 1981

Main theme: Structural design change, load path, professional responsibility, communication failure, engineer's seal, and public safety in a crowded building.

Collapse Narrative

1. The Case Story

In 1981, the Hyatt Regency Hotel in Kansas City was hosting a crowded tea dance in its atrium. More than 2,000 people were inside. Two suspended walkways collapsed into the lobby, killing 114 people and injuring more than 200. There was no earthquake or explosion. The walkways fell because of decisions made during design and construction.

The Hyatt atrium was designed to impress. Skywalks crossed the open lobby at different levels, creating visual movement and drama. The project was also under schedule pressure, and fast-track construction was used, meaning design and construction overlapped. This saved time but left less room for careful checking before decisions moved into the built structure.

The original structural concept used continuous hanger rods running from the ceiling down through the upper walkway to support the lower walkway. The fabricator, Haven Steel, found this difficult to build and proposed splitting the rods into two separate pieces. The change looked like a minor construction adjustment. It was not.

Original design

Continuous rod from ceiling, through upper walkway, down to lower walkway. Each walkway carried its own load along the full rod.

Changed design

Two separate rods. Upper rod ceiling to upper walkway. Lower rod from upper walkway down. The upper connection now carried both walkways, doubling the load.

The upper walkway connection was already below code strength, around 60 percent of what was required. After the rod change, it had to carry twice the load. Capacity dropped to around 30 percent of what the code required. During the dance, the connection failed. The upper walkway crashed into the lower walkway and both fell into the crowded lobby.

The ethical issue is that the change was never properly recalculated, reviewed, or controlled. The structural engineer's seal implied responsibility for the drawings. The shop drawing change was accepted without recalculation. A roof collapse during construction had already shown warning signs, and a broader independent review was offered but not fully used. The walkway danger was never caught.
