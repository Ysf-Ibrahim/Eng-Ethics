Case 1 of 10

Volkswagen Emissions Scandal

Main theme: Honesty, integrity, deceptive software, environmental responsibility, legal compliance, accountability, and public trust.

Lesson ready

Case Story

1. Case Story

Volkswagen promoted some of its diesel vehicles as clean, efficient, and environmentally friendly. Customers, regulators, and the public were led to believe that these vehicles met emissions standards while still offering strong performance.

The ethical problem was that Volkswagen installed defeat-device software. This software could detect when the vehicle was being tested under official laboratory conditions. During the test, the vehicle adjusted its behavior to appear compliant with emissions rules. But during normal real-world driving, the vehicles produced emissions much higher than the legal limit.

This made the scandal more than a normal technical defect. The software worked as intended, but its purpose was unethical. It used engineering and programming knowledge to mislead regulators and hide the real environmental impact of the vehicles.

The scandal became visible when real-world emissions results did not match laboratory results. Independent testing and regulators helped reveal that the vehicles were cleaner during official tests than during normal driving. This showed that the issue was not random failure, but a deliberate gap between test behavior and real-world behavior.

Volkswagen's actions violated honesty, transparency, legal compliance, environmental responsibility, professional responsibility, and public trust. The consequences included fines, legal cases, recalls, compensation, leadership changes, and major reputation damage.

This is an engineering ethics case because it shows that technical skill must be guided by ethical responsibility. Engineers must not use software to deceive. Engineering success is not only passing a test; it is protecting the public, the environment, and trust in the profession.

Heathrow Terminal 5 Opening Disaster

Main theme: Professional responsibility, operational readiness, stakeholder management, risk and crisis management, communication, accountability, and public trust in complex public systems.

Lesson ready

Case Story

1. Case Story

Heathrow Terminal 5 opened in 2008 as one of the largest and most complex airport projects in Europe. It was built at a cost of about 4.3 billion and involved a massive operational and technology environment, including many IT applications, vendors, interfaces, devices, and one of Europe's largest baggage-handling systems.

The terminal had been tested for months, but opening day did not go as planned. Passengers and staff had trouble finding car parks and reaching the terminal smoothly. Check-in counters opened late, queues built up, and the baggage system struggled. As the day continued, baggage handling failed badly, check-ins were suspended, some passengers flew without their luggage, and many flights were cancelled.

The failure was not caused by one small technical problem. It was a combination of operational, technical, communication, and management failures. Staff were not trained well enough for the real opening-day conditions. Some staff did not know how to handle baggage processes correctly. Passengers complained that communication was poor, with little clear information, help, or announcements.

The project also suffered from weak risk and crisis planning. For such a large public-facing system, the Big Bang approach of moving many operations into the terminal at once created high risk. A phased launch would have allowed the organization to detect problems with fewer passengers and less disruption. There were also performance and capacity issues in the baggage information system, including a testing software filter that was not removed and server capacity that could not handle the opening-day load.

The ethical issue is that Heathrow T5 was not only a project management failure. It was an engineering ethics case because professionals have a duty to launch systems only when they are operationally ready, to listen to stakeholder concerns, to communicate honestly, and to protect the public from foreseeable disruption and harm.

The main lesson is that complex engineering systems must be managed with honesty, realistic risk assessment, proper training, strong communication, stakeholder involvement, and contingency planning. A successful project is not successful only because the building is complete. It must work safely, reliably, and responsibly for the people who depend on it.

Boeing 737 MAX Crisis

Main theme: Safety-critical engineering, MCAS, insufficient transparency, weak pilot training, certification/oversight concerns, competition pressure, and public safety.

Lesson ready

Safety-critical engineering

1. The Case Story

Boeing developed the 737 MAX under strong pressure to compete with Airbus. Instead of designing a completely new aircraft, Boeing updated the older 737 design by adding larger fuel-efficient engines. Because the 737 was lower to the ground, the larger engines had to be placed higher and farther forward on the wings. This changed how the aircraft behaved during certain flight conditions and could make the nose pitch upward.

To handle this behavior, Boeing added MCAS, the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System. MCAS could automatically push the aircraft nose down if the system believed the plane was climbing too steeply. The ethical issue was not simply that Boeing used software. The issue was that MCAS affected flight control, but many pilots were not clearly informed about it or trained deeply enough to understand how it could behave in an emergency.

Boeing also marketed the 737 MAX as very similar to earlier 737 models. This helped reduce costly pilot retraining and made the aircraft more attractive to airlines. However, this created an ethical conflict: business pressure and competition pushed Boeing to minimize how different the MAX really was, even though MCAS introduced safety-critical behavior.

The problem became visible after two similar fatal crashes: Lion Air Flight 610 and Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302. In both cases, the aircraft experienced dangerous nose-down behavior shortly after takeoff. Faulty sensor input could activate MCAS, and pilots struggled against a system they were not fully prepared to handle.

The crisis led to global grounding of the 737 MAX, investigations, lawsuits and settlements, leadership changes, major damage to Boeing's reputation, and serious questions about FAA certification and Boeing's safety culture.

This is an engineering ethics case because design, software, training, communication, certification, and business decisions affected human lives. The main lesson is that in safety-critical engineering, public safety, transparency, training, accountability, and honest risk communication must come before profit, speed, and competition.

Safety-critical software

1. The Case Story

Launch and failure: Ariane 5 Flight 501 was launched by the European Space Agency in 1996. Shortly after liftoff, the rocket began receiving invalid guidance data, moved away from its intended flight path, experienced severe stress, and was destroyed.

Software fault: The failure came from software inside the rocket's inertial reference system, called the SRI. The software attempted to convert a large 64-bit floating-point value into a 16-bit signed integer. The value was too large for the smaller data type, causing overflow. This produced invalid data, but the flight computer treated it as real guidance information and attempted to correct the rocket's trajectory.

Software reuse: Ariane 4 software was reused in Ariane 5 because Ariane 4 had worked successfully for years. However, Ariane 5 was not the same system. It had different flight behavior and could produce values outside the safe range expected by the old software. Testing failed to catch the problem because it did not properly test realistic Ariane 5 flight conditions or enough overflow and boundary cases.

Common-mode failure: The backup system also failed because it used the same software as the primary SRI. When the primary SRI failed, the backup repeated the same fault. This is called common-mode failure. A backup is not truly protective if it fails for the same reason as the original system.

Main lesson: This is an engineering ethics case because software controlled a safety-critical physical system. The failure was not only a programming error. It reflected unsafe software reuse, weak validation, poor risk management, overconfidence in previous success, and inadequate redundancy. The main lesson is that reused software must be revalidated whenever the system, hardware, mission, or operating conditions change.

Case Story

1. The Case Story

Tacoma Narrows Bridge opened in 1940 as a long suspension bridge in Washington state. It was elegant, modern, and economical. To reduce material and cost, engineers used a slender plate-girder design instead of a deeper and stiffer truss system. This made the bridge visually graceful, but also flexible and vulnerable to wind-induced motion.

Even before the collapse, the bridge moved noticeably in moderate winds. Workers and the public nicknamed it "Galloping Gertie." This movement was a warning sign that the bridge was behaving unusually under real environmental conditions. The bridge did not fail because of an earthquake, explosion, or simple traffic overload. It failed because its design did not adequately handle dynamic wind effects.

A common misunderstanding is that Tacoma Narrows collapsed only because of resonance. The bridge did experience vertical oscillations connected to wind and vortex shedding, but the final collapse was mainly caused by torsional flutter. Torsional flutter means the bridge began twisting, and wind forces amplified that twisting until the structure could no longer survive.

The ethical issue is that public infrastructure must be safe under realistic conditions, not only under ideal calculations. The bridge may have looked efficient and strong enough for gravity loads, but it was not stable enough under wind. Cost-saving and innovation were not wrong by themselves, but they became risky because the design pushed limits without enough aerodynamic understanding and validation.

Tacoma Narrows is an engineering ethics case because it shows that engineers must consider all relevant loads, respond to warning signs, test uncertain designs, and protect public safety over cost, appearance, or confidence in traditional assumptions. The main lesson is that elegant and economical design is not enough if the structure is unsafe under real-world dynamic forces.

Original design

Continuous rod from ceiling, through upper walkway, down to lower walkway. Each walkway carried its own load along the full rod.

Two separate rods. Upper rod ceiling to upper walkway. Lower rod from upper walkway down. The upper connection now carried both walkways, doubling the load.

  1. What ethical principles were violated in the Theranos case, and how did these violations affect public trust in medical technology? ⭐ Highly Important

    Theranos violated honesty, integrity, public safety, transparency, professional responsibility, accountability, and public trust. Honesty was violated because the company claimed its blood-testing technology could do more than it actually could. Public safety was violated because inaccurate blood-test results could affect diagnosis, medication, and medical decisions. Transparency failed because patients, partners, investors, and regulators were not clearly told the true limitations of the technology. These failures damaged public trust because people expect medical technology to be accurate, validated, and honest.

    Key points
    • Honesty / integrity
    • Public safety
    • False claims
    • Unreliable results
    • Transparency
    • Public trust
    Best keywords

    honesty, patient safety, transparency, accountability, public trust

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not write only "the startup failed." Explain the ethical deception and patient risk.

  2. How did Theranos violate professional responsibility in medical technology? ⭐ Highly Important

    Professional responsibility was violated because Theranos promoted and used medical technology before it was properly validated. Blood-test results can influence real medical decisions, so the company had a duty to ensure accuracy, test reliability, disclose limitations, and avoid misleading claims. Instead, Theranos exaggerated what its devices could do and used third-party machines while implying its own technology worked. In medical technology, innovation is ethical only when it is supported by evidence and protects patients.

    Key points
    • Medical device validation
    • Accuracy
    • False claims
    • Third-party machines
    • Patient protection
    Best keywords

    professional responsibility, validation, medical technology, accuracy, patient welfare

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not treat medical devices like normal consumer gadgets.

  3. Explain how transparency and accountability failed in the Theranos case. ⭐ Highly Important

    Transparency failed because Theranos did not clearly disclose the real performance limits of its devices to patients, investors, partners, employees, or regulators. The company gave the impression that its own machines performed tests when many results were produced using third-party commercial machines. Accountability failed because leadership continued misleading claims, suppressed concerns, and resisted responsibility when problems appeared. Ethical accountability required admitting limitations, correcting claims, stopping unsafe use, and protecting patients.

    Key points
    • Hidden limitations
    • Third-party testing
    • Misleading partners/patients
    • Suppressed concerns
    • Need corrective action
    Best keywords

    transparency, accountability, disclosure, correction, patient safety

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not define transparency generally. Apply it to Theranos's hidden technology limits.

  4. What role did whistle-blowing play in exposing Theranos, and when is whistle-blowing ethically justified? ⭐ Highly Important

    Whistle-blowing was important because employees and insiders helped reveal that Theranos technology was unreliable and that the company was misleading stakeholders. Whistle-blowing is ethically justified when serious harm is possible, internal channels have been tried or ignored, evidence is documented, and disclosure is aimed at protecting the public. In Theranos, patient safety was at stake because inaccurate blood-test results could affect medical care. Whistleblowers helped restore accountability when internal culture discouraged honest reporting.

    Key points
    • Employees knew problems

    • Documented evidence
    • Patient safety
    • Public protection
    Best keywords

    whistle-blowing, evidence, internal channels, patient safety, public interest

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not say whistle-blowing is always disloyal. It can be a moral duty when serious harm is at stake.

  5. How did Theranos's toxic work culture contribute to the ethical failure? ⭐ Highly Important

    Theranos's toxic work culture contributed because employees who questioned the technology were pressured, silenced, fired, or made afraid through confidentiality agreements and retaliation. This prevented honest communication about technical problems and allowed deception to continue. A healthy engineering culture should allow employees to raise concerns, ask questions, and report safety risks without fear. When organizations punish truth-telling, technical problems can become ethical disasters.

  6. What is the main engineering ethics lesson from the Theranos case? ⭐ Highly Important

    The main lesson is that innovation must be controlled by honesty, validation, transparency, and patient safety. Theranos shows that impressive vision, famous investors, and media attention cannot replace scientific evidence. Engineers and leaders must be realistic about what technology can do, disclose limitations, protect patients, and build a culture where concerns can be raised safely. In medical technology, false claims can directly harm people and destroy public trust.
