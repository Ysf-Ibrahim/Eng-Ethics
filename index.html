Volkswagen promoted some diesel vehicles as clean and environmentally friendly, but secretly installed defeat-device software that detected emissions testing conditions. During lab tests, the vehicles appeared compliant, but during real road driving they produced much higher emissions, up to 40 times the allowable limit.

Why is it ethics?

Software and engineering knowledge were intentionally used to deceive regulators, governments, testers, and consumers. The system worked as designed, but the design purpose was unethical.

How was it discovered?

Real road emissions did not match laboratory emissions. European testers noticed the difference, and portable on-road testing gave scientific evidence of much higher real-driving emissions.

Principles violated

Honesty, transparency, integrity, legal compliance, environmental responsibility, public welfare, accountability, professional responsibility, and consumer rights.

Stakeholders

Consumers, regulators, governments, testers, the public, the environment, employees, shareholders, and the automobile industry.

Causes

Short-term profit pressure, market-share pressure, weak ethical culture, poor accountability, and hiding the emissions problem instead of solving it honestly.

Consequences

Criminal charges, financial penalties, compensation, buybacks/retrofits, leadership resignation, reputation damage, and loss of public trust.

What should have been done?

Design vehicles that truly met emissions standards, refuse deceptive software, listen to ethical concerns, report misconduct, cooperate with regulators, compensate consumers, and change corporate culture.

Volkswagen shows that engineering skill must be guided by ethics. Engineers must not use technical knowledge to deceive; they must protect public welfare, the environment, and public trust.

Opening-day story

1. The Opening Day Story

A modern terminal with a huge system behind it

Heathrow Terminal 5 was presented as a major modern airport terminal designed to offer luxury, style, and world-class service. It was a very large and complex project involving many IT applications, vendors, interfaces, computers, connected devices, and a huge baggage-handling system.

The opening day failed badly

When Terminal 5 opened in 2008, problems began early. Staff and passengers had difficulty finding car parks and navigating the new layout. Staff delays and confusion meant check-in counters opened late, long queues formed, and passengers waited for luggage processing.

The baggage system became the crisis point

The baggage handling system began to fail, conveyor belt problems appeared, and the system could not handle the baggage flow. Later in the day, baggage check-in was suspended and many passengers flew without their bags.

The consequences spread quickly

British Airways cancelled flights on opening day and more flights in the following days. A baggage backlog developed, passengers were frustrated, and the airline faced refunds, compensation costs, and reputation damage.

Core idea

This was not only an operational inconvenience. Terminal 5 was a public-facing engineering and project system. When a complex system launches without enough readiness, training, risk planning, communication, and stakeholder response, the public is harmed.

Opening-day timeline

Opening Day Failure Timeline

  1. Navigation confusion: Staff and passengers struggled to locate car parks and navigate the terminal.
  2. Late check-in: Check-in counters opened late because staff were delayed and confused.
  3. Queues formed: Passenger waiting times increased and pressure built across the terminal.
  4. Baggage issues appeared: Conveyor and baggage-system problems disrupted luggage flow.
  5. Baggage check-in suspended: The system could not process luggage properly.
  6. Passengers flew without luggage: Some flights departed without passengers' bags.
  7. Flights were cancelled: British Airways cancelled flights on opening day and after.
  8. Aftermath followed: Compensation, reputation damage, and inquiry followed the failure.

A+ Written Answer Summary

Heathrow Terminal 5 was a huge and complex airport terminal project. It was expected to provide modern, world-class service, but its opening day failed badly. Staff and passengers struggled with car parks, navigation, late check-in, long queues, baggage-system problems, cancelled flights, and passengers flying without luggage.

Why is it ethics?

A public-facing system was launched before it was truly operationally ready. The failure affected passengers, staff, public trust, service reliability, and the reputation of British Airways and Heathrow.

How did it become visible?

Real opening-day conditions exposed problems that testing and planning had not solved. Baggage handling failed, check-in was disrupted, communication was poor, and flights were cancelled.

Principles involved

Risk management, crisis management, stakeholder management, communication, accountability, operational readiness, professional responsibility, and public welfare.

Stakeholders

Passengers, staff, British Airways, airport management, regulators, airlines, shareholders, and the public.

Causes

Big Bang launch approach, weak risk and crisis planning, insufficient staff training, poor communication, baggage-system and capacity problems, ignored staff concerns, and deadline pressure.

Consequences

Passenger inconvenience, missing luggage, cancelled flights, refunds, compensation, public inquiry, reputation damage, and loss of trust.

What should have been done?

Use a phased rollout, train staff better, test under realistic full-load conditions, listen to staff warnings, prepare contingency plans, improve communication, and delay launch if readiness was not enough.

Final lesson sentence

Heathrow Terminal 5 shows that opening on time is not success if the system is not operationally ready for the people who depend on it.

Why it is ethics

2. Why This Became an Ethics Case

This case is ethical because engineering and project decisions affected the public directly. The failure caused passenger inconvenience, long queues, missing baggage, cancelled flights, poor communication, loss of trust, financial costs, and reputation damage.

The ethical issue was not intentional fraud like Volkswagen. Instead, the ethical concern was negligence or poor responsibility in project execution. A project can be technically impressive but still ethically weak if it launches before it is operationally ready.

Failure map

4. Main Failure Areas

01

Big Bang Launch Approach

British Airways moved many operations into the new terminal at once. When problems started, there was little room to recover. A phased launch with fewer flights first would have reduced risk.

Ethics meaning:

A large public system should not expose passengers to avoidable disruption when a safer phased approach is possible.

02

Weak Risk and Crisis Management

⭐ Most Important

The baggage system failure, queues, car park confusion, security screening delays, and layout confusion were not handled well. Contingency planning and crisis response were weak.

Ethics meaning:

Organizations have a duty to plan for predictable failures, especially in public infrastructure projects.

03

Poor Communication

Passengers reported little help, poor announcements, and weak information on monitors or websites.

Ethics meaning:

When users are affected, communication is not optional. It is part of accountability and respect for stakeholders.

04

Insufficient Training and Logistics Preparation

Staff were not prepared enough for the new terminal's operations. Some staff reportedly did not know how to unload processed baggage properly, contributing to overload and confusion.

Ethics meaning:

A system is not ready if the people operating it are not ready.

05

Implementation and Performance Planning Problems

Issues included software filter problems, message/server capacity limitations, and baggage testing that did not fully represent opening-day capacity.

Ethics meaning:

Testing must represent real operating conditions, not only controlled scenarios.

06

Ignored Stakeholder Concerns

Staff and unions raised concerns that the terminal was not ready, but these concerns were ignored.

Ethics meaning:

Ethical project management requires listening to people who understand and will operate the system.

07

External Pressure and Public Perception

Management felt pressure to open on the planned deadline and improve public perception of Heathrow and British Airways. This pressure contributed to opening despite risks.

Ethics meaning:

Deadlines and reputation should not override readiness and public responsibility.

Six lessons

Six Lessons from Heathrow T5

1. Avoid Big Bang launch for huge systems.
2. Plan risks and crisis response.
3. Train staff before launch.
4. Test performance under realistic load.
5. Listen to stakeholder concerns.
6. Do not let deadline/public-image pressure override readiness.

Boeing 737 MAX Crisis

Main theme: Safety-critical engineering, MCAS, insufficient transparency, weak pilot training, certification/oversight concerns, competition pressure, and public safety.

Lesson ready

Safety-critical engineering

1. Story

Boeing developed the 737 MAX under strong pressure to compete with Airbus, especially after Airbus introduced a more fuel-efficient aircraft that required little extra pilot training. Boeing chose to update the older 737 design by adding larger fuel-efficient engines instead of designing a completely new aircraft. However, because the 737 sat lower to the ground, the larger engines had to be placed higher and farther forward on the wing. This changed how the aircraft behaved during takeoff and high thrust, creating a tendency for the nose to pitch upward.

To solve this, Boeing added MCAS, the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System. MCAS could automatically push the aircraft nose down if the system believed the plane was climbing at too steep an angle. The ethical issue was not simply that Boeing used software. The issue was that MCAS was safety-critical, but Boeing still presented the 737 MAX as very similar to earlier 737 models. Many pilots received minimal training, and MCAS was not clearly highlighted in training materials.

The crisis became visible after two similar fatal crashes: Lion Air Flight 610 and Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302. In both cases, the aircraft experienced dangerous nose-down behavior shortly after takeoff. Faulty sensor data could trigger MCAS, and pilots struggled to understand and fight the system. Investigations later focused on MCAS design, weak pilot training, Boeing's transparency with pilots and regulators, FAA certification/oversight, and whether Boeing's safety culture had been weakened by business pressure.

The case is an engineering ethics case because aircraft software and design decisions directly affect human lives. Boeing's competition with Airbus, desire to avoid costly retraining, and pressure to sell the aircraft quickly contributed to decisions that minimized safety-critical changes. The main lesson is that in safety-critical engineering, profit, competition, and certification are not enough. Companies must prioritize safety, transparency, training, accountability, and public trust.

Ariane 5 Flight 501 Software Failure

Main theme: Software failure in a safety-critical system, code reuse without proper validation, insufficient testing, overconfidence, poor redundancy, and professional responsibility in software engineering.

Lesson ready

Safety-critical software

1. Story

Ariane 5 Flight 501 was launched by the European Space Agency in 1996. Shortly after liftoff, the rocket malfunctioned, deviated from its intended flight path, experienced extreme stress, and was destroyed. The payload was not the main cause of the disaster. The failure came from software inside the rocket's inertial reference system.

The key background is that Ariane 4 had been successful for many years, so parts of its software were reused in Ariane 5. However, Ariane 5 was not simply another Ariane 4. It had different hardware, different flight behavior, and could produce values outside the safe range expected by the old software. The team became overconfident because the old software had worked before.

The technical failure came from a data conversion problem. A large 64-bit floating-point value was converted into a 16-bit signed integer. The value was too large for the smaller data type, causing overflow and corrupted data. The flight computer then used this invalid data and tried to correct the rocket's trajectory incorrectly, which caused the rocket to tilt and fail.

Testing did not catch the problem because the tests did not use realistic Ariane 5 flight values large enough to trigger the overflow. The code had been safe in Ariane 4 because Ariane 4 did not produce values that exceeded the old range, but Ariane 5 did.

The backup system also failed because it used the same identical software as the primary system. This means the backup repeated the same software fault instead of protecting against it. The redundancy was useful against some hardware failures, but weak against common-mode software failure.

This is an engineering ethics case because safety-critical software must be revalidated whenever the system changes. The disaster was not only a small programming bug. It reflected unsafe code reuse, unrealistic testing, weak redundancy, poor risk management, and an organizational culture that trusted past success too much. The main lesson is that reused software is not automatically safe in a new system. Engineers must test real conditions, check boundary cases, handle failures safely, and question old assumptions.

The case is not only about one coding bug; it is about unsafe software reuse, overconfidence, unrealistic testing, weak redundancy, and failure to question old assumptions in a safety-critical system.

Without proper revalidation, the reused code became unsafe in the new environment.

    Key points
    • Different hardware/flight conditions
    • Old assumptions no longer valid
    • Larger values
    • Revalidation needed
    Best keywords

    code reuse, assumptions, changed context, validation, overflow

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not say old successful code is automatically safe.

  3. What ethical principles were violated in the Ariane 5 case? ⭐ Highly Important

    The main ethical principles involved were professional responsibility, testing and validation, risk management, redundancy responsibility, accountability, and public trust. Professional responsibility was the most important because engineers had a duty to verify that reused software was safe for Ariane 5. Testing was insufficient because it did not include realistic Ariane 5 values. Risk management was weak because the backup system used the same software and failed in the same way.

    Key points
    • Professional responsibility
    • Testing/validation
    • Risk management
    • Backup failure
    • Accountability
    Best keywords

    professional responsibility, validation, testing, risk management, redundancy

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not list principles without explaining how they were violated.

  4. How did the Ariane 5 failure become visible, and why is this ethically important? ⭐ Highly Important

    The failure became visible shortly after launch when the rocket deviated from its intended path and was destroyed. This is ethically important because the visible explosion was caused by preventable software and validation failures. The case shows that software errors in safety-critical systems can cause major physical and financial consequences. Engineers must identify these risks before launch, not after failure.

    Key points
    • Shortly after launch
    • Rocket deviated
    • Destroyed
    • Preventable software failure
    • Real-world consequence
    Best keywords

    failure visibility, launch failure, validation, software failure, physical consequence

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not say it was discovered only through a normal code review.

  5. Why was testing insufficient in the Ariane 5 case? ⭐ Highly Important

    Testing was insufficient because it did not represent Ariane 5's real flight conditions and did not include values large enough to reveal the overflow problem. The software worked with smaller values, so the error was missed. In safety-critical systems, testing must include realistic scenarios, boundary values, and failure conditions, especially when software is reused in a new system.

    Key points
    • Real conditions not tested
    • Boundary values missed
    • Overflow not detected
    • Reused software
    Best keywords

    testing, validation, boundary values, overflow, real conditions

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not say testing is useless. The issue is insufficient testing.

  6. What was wrong with the backup system in Ariane 5? ⭐ Highly Important

    The backup inertial reference system used the same identical software as the primary system. When the primary failed because of the overflow problem, the backup failed in the same way. This is called a common-mode failure. A backup system should reduce risk, but identical software repeated the same error, so redundancy did not protect the rocket from software failure.

    Key points
    • Identical software
    • Same failure repeated
    • Common-mode failure
    • Weak redundancy
    Best keywords

    redundancy, backup system, common-mode failure, software fault

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not assume any backup automatically guarantees safety.

  7. How did organizational culture contribute to the Ariane 5 disaster? ⭐ Highly Important

    Organizational culture contributed because the team was overconfident due to Ariane 4's long success. They assumed the old software would continue to work on Ariane 5 and focused more on hardware reliability than software failure modes. This culture undervalued software testing, boundary cases, and changed assumptions. In safety-critical projects, culture must encourage questioning assumptions and testing failure conditions.

    Key points
    • Overconfidence
    • Past success
    • Hardware-focused mindset
    • Software testing undervalued
    • Assumptions not questioned
    Best keywords

    organizational culture, overconfidence, assumptions, software testing, safety

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not blame only one programmer.

  8. What should engineers have done to prevent the Ariane 5 failure? ⭐ Highly Important

    Engineers should have revalidated Ariane 4 software under Ariane 5's real flight conditions, tested boundary and overflow cases, improved exception handling, reviewed assumptions from the old system, and designed backup systems that would not fail in the same way. They should have treated Ariane 5 as a new safety-critical environment rather than trusting old software success.

    Key points
    • Revalidate code
    • Test real values
    • Boundary/overflow testing
    • Exception handling
    • Diverse redundancy
    Best keywords

    prevention, validation, boundary testing, redundancy, safety-critical software

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not only say "fix the bug."

  9. Who were the stakeholders affected by the Ariane 5 failure?

    The stakeholders included the European Space Agency, engineers, satellite owners, funders or taxpayers, future space missions, and the public. The failure caused financial loss, mission failure, reputation damage, and reduced confidence in safety-critical software and space engineering.

    Key points
    • ESA
    • Engineers
    • Satellite owners
    • Funders
    • Public trust
    Best keywords

    stakeholders, ESA, funders, mission failure, public trust

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not say there were no stakeholders because no passengers died.

  10. What is the main engineering ethics lesson from Ariane 5?

    The main lesson is that reused software is not automatically safe in a new system. In safety-critical engineering, every change in hardware, environment, or mission conditions requires new testing and validation. Engineers must check assumptions, handle overflow and failures safely, design proper redundancy, and take responsibility for software that controls physical systems.

    Key points
    • Reused software needs validation
    • Changed system means changed risk
    • Realistic testing
    • Fail-safe design
    • Responsibility
    Best keywords

    safety-critical software, validation, code reuse, testing, professional responsibility

    Mistake to avoid

    Do not treat it as only a technical bug.
